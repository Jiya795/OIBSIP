The Journey

From a Small Classroom in Karnal
to the Flight Deck of Columbia

Kalpana Chawla was born in 1962 in Karnal, a town in present-day Haryana, where she grew up captivated by the sight of aircraft overhead. That early fascination turned into a clear ambition: she wanted to study aeronautical engineering, a field almost no women in India were pursuing at the time. Professors at Punjab Engineering College tried to steer her elsewhere, pointing out how few opportunities existed for women in the industry. She stayed the course anyway, and graduated in 1982.

She then moved to the United States for graduate study, earning a master's degree in aerospace engineering from the University of Texas at Arlington and a PhD from the University of Colorado Boulder in 1988. She became a naturalized U.S. citizen in 1991. Her research career began at NASA's Ames Research Center, where she worked on computational fluid dynamics — the science of modeling how air flows around aircraft — before joining Overset Methods Inc. as a vice president and research scientist.

In 1994, NASA selected her for astronaut training out of a pool of nearly 4,000 applicants. She reported to the Johnson Space Center the following year, and by 1997 she was in space — a mission specialist and robotic arm operator aboard Space Shuttle Columbia's STS-87 flight. It made her the first Indian-born woman ever to travel to space. Over sixteen days, the shuttle circled the Earth 252 times.

Her second mission came in January 2003, again aboard Columbia, for a sixteen-day flight dedicated to more than eighty scientific experiments. On February 1, as the shuttle re-entered Earth's atmosphere, damage sustained during launch caused it to break apart, killing all seven crew members. Chawla was forty years old.

Legacy

Still Orbiting, in More Ways Than One

Chawla's name now belongs to more than her own story. An asteroid and a set of Martian hills carry her name, as does a NASA cargo spacecraft. Universities in India and the United States have dedicated scholarships, halls, and memorials to her. But those closest to her point to something simpler: her insistence, throughout her career, that young girls — in her hometown and beyond — be given the same chance she once had to fight for.
